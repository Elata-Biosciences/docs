## What Is EEG?

**Electroencephalography (EEG)** is a non-invasive method of recording electrical activity produced by the brain. Neurons communicate using electrical signals, and when large populations of neurons fire in synchrony, the resulting electrical field is strong enough to be detected through the scalp using small electrodes.

The signal captured by EEG is a time-varying voltage, typically in the range of **10–100 microvolts (μV)**, sampled many times per second.

---

## Brainwave Frequency Bands

The EEG signal is usually analyzed in terms of **frequency bands**, each associated with different mental states:

| Band | Frequency | Associated State |
|---|---|---|
| **Delta** | 0.5 – 4 Hz | Deep sleep, unconscious processing |
| **Theta** | 4 – 8 Hz | Drowsiness, memory consolidation, creativity |
| **Alpha** | 8 – 13 Hz | Relaxed wakefulness, closed eyes, calm focus |
| **Beta** | 13 – 30 Hz | Active thinking, problem solving, alertness |
| **Gamma** | 30 – 100 Hz | High-level cognition, perception, learning |

<Tip>
  **Alpha waves** are the most commonly targeted in consumer apps. When you close your eyes and relax, alpha power increases dramatically — this is easy to detect reliably even with consumer-grade EEG.
</Tip>

## Consumer EEG Devices

Clinical EEG uses 64–256 electrodes placed precisely on the scalp. Consumer devices simplify this dramatically — typically 4 electrodes — which reduces signal quality but makes the technology wearable and affordable.

The **Muse headband** (Muse 2, Muse S) is the primary consumer device supported by Elata's SDK:

| Device | Channels | Sample Rate | Notable Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Muse 2 | 4 EEG (TP9, AF7, AF8, TP10) | 256 Hz | EEG + PPG + accelerometer |
| Muse S | 4 EEG + PPG | 256 Hz | Softer headband for sleep |
| Muse S (Athena) | 8 EEG + optics | 256 Hz | Higher channel count, new firmware |

<Note>
  The 4 EEG channels on a Muse headband cover the temporal lobes (TP9, TP10) and frontal cortex (AF7, AF8). This placement is sufficient for detecting alpha activity and basic attention metrics, but not for fine-grained spatial localization.
</Note>

## Signal Processing Pipeline

Raw EEG data is noisy. Before it's useful, it goes through a processing pipeline:

```
Raw EEG samples (256 Hz)
    │
    ▼
Artifact Removal
(remove muscle, blink, movement noise)
    │
    ▼
Bandpass Filtering
(isolate frequency ranges of interest)
    │
    ▼
FFT / Power Spectral Density
(convert time signal → frequency domain)
    │
    ▼
Band Power Extraction
(alpha power, beta power, etc.)
    │
    ▼
Model / Classifier
(e.g. Calmness Model, Alpha Bump Detector)
    │
    ▼
App-facing output
(score, state, event trigger)
```

The Elata EEG SDK (`@elata-biosciences/eeg-web`) handles every stage of this pipeline in WebAssembly, so developers get clean, meaningful outputs rather than raw voltages.

## Limitations to Know

EEG is powerful but has real constraints that matter for app development:

- **Noise** — muscle artifacts, eye blinks, and electrode movement contaminate the signal
- **Spatial resolution** — 4 electrodes can't pinpoint exactly which brain region is active
- **Individual variation** — alpha frequency and amplitude vary significantly between people; calibration helps
- **Impedance** — dry electrodes (no conductive gel) have higher impedance, affecting signal quality

The Elata SDK includes built-in models that account for these constraints in practical ways.
