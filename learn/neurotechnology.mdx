## What Is Neurotechnology?

Neurotechnology refers to tools and devices that interact directly with the nervous system — measuring, interpreting, or influencing brain and body signals. It sits at the intersection of neuroscience, engineering, and software.

Modern consumer neurotechnology typically falls into two broad categories:

| Category | What It Measures | Example Devices |
|---|---|---|
| **EEG** (electroencephalography) | Electrical brain activity via scalp electrodes | Muse 2, Muse S |
| **rPPG** (remote photoplethysmography) | Heart rate from subtle skin color changes on camera | Any webcam |

---

## Why Does It Matter?

The brain generates continuous streams of data that reflect how you think, feel, focus, and recover. Until recently, meaningful access to this data required clinical settings and expensive equipment.

Consumer-grade neurotechnology changes this. With a $200 EEG headband and a laptop camera, anyone can now measure:

- **Focus and attention** — how well your brain is engaged with a task
- **Relaxation and stress** — alpha wave patterns correlate with calm states
- **Heart rate variability** — a key indicator of stress, recovery, and autonomic balance
- **Cognitive fatigue** — changes in brainwave patterns over time

<Info>
  This is early-stage technology. Signal quality from consumer devices is lower than clinical EEG, but sufficient for many real-world applications.
</Info>

## The Missing Layer: Open Infrastructure

Despite the hardware existing, building neurotechnology applications has been extremely difficult:

- **No open SDKs** — each device has proprietary, closed tooling
- **No shared data standards** — researchers can't compare results across devices
- **No economic model** — no way to reward developers who build biometric apps
- **No permissionless research** — academic data collection requires complex IRB approvals and centralized gatekeepers

**Elata is the infrastructure layer that fixes this.** It provides open SDKs for brain and body signals, an on-chain app store for biometric apps, and a protocol that lets anyone collect, contribute, and monetize neuroscience data.

## Who Is Elata For?

**Developers** can build brain-powered games, focus trackers, meditation apps, or any experience that uses real biometric data — and launch it on the Elata App Store with its own token economy.

**Researchers** can collect and publish neurological datasets without centralized approval using ZORP, the permissionless research protocol.

**Users** can experience apps that respond to their actual brain and body state, stake ELTA to earn rewards, and participate in governance over the protocol.
